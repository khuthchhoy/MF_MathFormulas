  • Arguments (independent variables): $x, y$
  • Functions of two variables: $f(x,y), M(x,y), N(x,y)$

  • Second order derivatives: $y^{\prime\prime}, \ddot{y}, \frac{d^{2}I}{dt^{2}}$
  • Partial derivatives: $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}, \frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial x^{2}}$
  • Natural number: $n$
  • Particular solutions: $y_{1}, Y_{p}$
  • Real numbers: $k, t, C, C_{1}, C_{2}, P, q, \alpha, \beta$
  • Roots of the characteristic equations: $\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}$
  • Time: $t$
  • Temperature: $T, S$
  • Population function: $P(t)$
  • Mass of an object: $m$
  • Stiffness of a spring: $k$

First Order Ordinary Differential Equations

  1. Linear Equations: $$\dfrac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = q(x)$$ The general solution is $$y = \dfrac{\int u(x)q(x)dx + C}{u(x)}$$ Where $u(x) = \exp(\int p(x)dx)$
  2. Separable Equations: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y) = g(x)h(y)$$ The general solution is given by $$\int \frac{dy}{h(y)} = \int g(x)dx + C$$ Alternative separable form: $H(y) = G(x) + C$
  3. Homogeneous Equations
    The differential equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x,y)$ is homogeneous, if the function $f(x,y)$ is homogeneous, that is $f(tx,ty)=f(x,y)$
    The substitution $z=\dfrac{y}{x}$ (then $y=zx$) leads to the separable equation $$x\frac{dz}{dx}+z=f(1,z)$$
  4. Bernoulli Equation $$\dfrac{dy}{dx}+p(x)y=q(x)y^{n}$$ The substitution $z=y^{1-n}$ leads to the linear equation $$\dfrac{dz}{dx}+(1-n)p(x)z=(1-n)q(x)$$
  5. Riccati Equation $$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=p(x)+q(x)y+r(x)y^{2}$$ If a particular solution $y_{1}$ is known, then the general solution can be obtained with the help of substitution $z=\dfrac{1}{y-y_{1}}$
    This leads to the first order linear equation $$\dfrac{dz}{dx}=-[q(x)+2y_{1}r(x)]z-r(x)$$
  6. Exact and Nonexact Equations
    The equation $M(x,y)dx+N(x,y)dy=0$ is called exact if $$\dfrac{\partial M}{\partial y}=\dfrac{\partial N}{\partial x}$$ and nonexact otherwise.
    The general solution is $$\int M(x,y)dx+\int N(x,y)dy=C$$
  7. Radioactive Decay $$\dfrac{dy}{dt}=-ky$$ where $y(t)$ is the amount of radioactive element at time $t$, and $k$ is the rate of decay.
    The solution is $$y(t)=y_{0}e^{-kt}$$ where $y_{0}=y(0)$ is the initial amount
  8. Newton's Law of Cooling $$\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-S)$$ where $T(t)$ is the temperature of an object at time $t$, $S$ is the temperature of the surrounding environment, and $k$ is a positive constant.
    The solution is $$T(t)=S+(T_{0}-S)e^{-kt}$$ where $T_{0}=T(0)$ is the initial temperature of the object at time $t=0$.
  9. Population Dynamics (Logistic Model) $$\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP(1-\dfrac{P}{M})$$ where $P(t)$ is population at time $t$, $k$ is a positive constant, and $M$ is a limiting size for the population.
    The solution of the differential equation is: $$P(t)=\dfrac{MP_{0}}{P_{0}+(M-P_{0})e^{-kt}}$$ where $P_{0}=P(0)$ is the initial population at time $t=0$.

Second Order Ordinary Differential Equations

  1. Homogeneous Linear Equations with Constant Coefficients $$y^{\prime\prime}+py^{\prime}+qy=0$$ The characteristic equation is: $$\lambda^{2}+p\lambda+q=0$$ If $\lambda_{1}$ and $\lambda_{2}$ are distinct real roots of the characteristic equation, then the general solution is: $$y=C_{1}e^{({\lambda_{1}x})}+C_{2}e^{({\lambda_{2}x})}$$ where $C_{1}$ and $C_{2}$ are integration constants.
    If $\lambda_{1}=\lambda_{2}=-\dfrac{p}{2}$, then the general solution is: $$y=(C_{1}+C_{2}x)e^{-\dfrac{p}{2}x}$$ If $\lambda_{1}$ and $\lambda_{2}$ are complex numbers:
    $\lambda_{1}=\alpha+\beta i, \lambda_{2}=\alpha-\beta i$ , where $\alpha=-\dfrac{p}{2}$ and $\beta=\dfrac{\sqrt{4q-p^{2}}}{2}$.
    The general solution is: $$y=e^{\alpha x}(C_{1}\cos \beta x+C_{2}\sin \beta x)$$
  2. Inhomogeneous Linear Equations with Constant Coefficients
    $$y^{\prime\prime}+py^{\prime}+qy=f(x)$$ The general solution is given by $y=y_{p}+y_{h}$, where $y_{p}$ is a particular solution and $y_{h}$ is the general solution of the associated homogeneous equation.
    If the right side has the form $$f(x)=e^{\alpha x}(P_{1}(x)\cos \beta x+P_{2}(x)\sin \beta x)$$ then the particular solution $y_{p}$ is: $$y_{p}=x^{k}e^{\alpha x}(R_{1}(x)\cos\beta x+R_{2}(x)\sin \beta x)$$ where $R_{1}(x)$ and $R_{2}(x)$ are found using the method of undetermined coefficients.
    The power $k$ is determined as follows:
    • $k=0$ if $\alpha+\beta i$ is not a root of the characteristic equation.
    • $k=1$ if $\alpha+\beta i$ is a simple root.
    • $k=2$ if $\alpha+\beta i$ is a double root.
  3. Differential Equations with $y$ Missing $$y^{\prime\prime}=f(x,y^{\prime})$$ Set $u=y^{\prime}$. Then the new equation satisfied by $u$ is: $$u^{\prime}=f(x,u)$$ which is a first order differential equation.
  4. Differential Equations with $x$ Missing $$y^{\prime\prime}=f(y,y^{\prime})$$ Set $u=y^{\prime}$. Since $y^{\prime\prime}=\dfrac{du}{dx}=\dfrac{du}{dy}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=u\dfrac{du}{dy}$, we have: $$u\dfrac{du}{dy}=f(y,u)$$ which is a first order differential equation.
  5. Free Undamped Vibrations
    The motion of a Mass on a Spring is described by the equation: $$m\ddot{y}+ky=0$$ where $m$ is the mass of the object, $k$ is the stiffness of the spring, and $y$ is displacement of the mass from equilibrium.
    The general solution is: $$y=A\cos(\omega_{0}t-\delta)$$ where $A$ is the amplitude of the displacement, $\omega_{0}$ is the fundamental frequency, $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega_{0}}$ is the period, and $\delta$ is the phase angle.
    This is an example of simple harmonic motion.
  6. 1178. Free Damped Vibrations $$m\ddot{y}+\gamma\dot{y}+ky=0$$ where $\gamma$ is the damping coefficient.
    There are 3 cases for the general solution.
    Case 1. $\gamma^{2}>4km$ (overdamped): $$y(t)=Ae^{\lambda_{1}t}+Be^{\lambda_{2}t}$$ where $\lambda_{1}=\dfrac{-\gamma-\sqrt{\gamma^{2}-4km}}{2m}$ and $\lambda_{2}=\dfrac{-\gamma+\sqrt{\gamma^{2}-4km}}{2m}$
    Case 2. $\gamma^{2}=4km$ (critically damped): $$y(t)=(A+Bt)e^{\lambda t}$$ where $\lambda=-\dfrac{\gamma}{2m}$.
    Case 3. $\gamma^{2}<4km$ (underdamped): $$y(t)=e^{\frac{\gamma}{2m}t}A\cos(\omega t-\delta)$$ where $\omega=\sqrt{4km-\gamma^{2}}$.
  7. Simple Pendulum $$\dfrac{d^{2}\theta}{dt^{2}}+\dfrac{g}{L}\theta=0$$ where $\theta$ is the angular displacement, $L$ is the pendulum length, and $g$ is the acceleration of gravity.
    The general solution for small angles is: $$\theta(t)=\theta_{max}\sin\sqrt{\dfrac{g}{L}t}$$ The period is $T=2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{g}}$.
  8. RLC Circuit $$L\dfrac{d^{2}I}{dt^{2}}+R\dfrac{dI}{dt}+\dfrac{1}{C}I=V^{\prime}(t)=\omega E_{0}\cos(\omega t)$$ Where $I$ is the current in an RLC circuit with an ac voltage source $V(t)=E_{0}\sin(\omega t)$.
    The general solution is: $$I(t)=C_{1}e^{r_{1}t}+C_{2}e^{r_{2}t}+A\sin(\omega t-\varphi)$$ Where: $$r_{1,2}=\dfrac{-R\pm\sqrt{R^{2}-\dfrac{4L}{C}}}{2L}$$ $$A=\dfrac{\omega E_{0}}{\sqrt{(L\omega^{2}-\dfrac{1}{C})^{2}+R^{2}\omega^{2}}}$$ $$\varphi=\arctan\left(\dfrac{L\omega}{R}-\dfrac{1}{RC\omega}\right)$$ $C_{1}$ and $C_{2}$ are constants depending on initial conditions.

Some Partial Differential Equations

  1. The Laplace Equation $$\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial x^{2}}+\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}}=0$$ This applies to potential energy function $u(x,y)$ for a conservative force field in the $xy$-plane.
    Partial differential equations of this type are called elliptic.
  2. The Heat Equation $$\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial x^{2}}+\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}}=\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}$$ This applies to the temperature distribution $u(x,y)$ in the $xy$-plane when heat is allowed to flow from warm areas to cool ones.
    Equations of this type are called parabolic.
  3. The Wave Equation
    $$\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial x^{2}}+\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial y^{2}}=\dfrac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial t^{2}}$$ This applies to the displacement $u(x,y)$ of vibrating membranes and other wave functions.
    Equations of this type are called hyperbolic.
