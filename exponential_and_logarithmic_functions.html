Laws of Exponents

In the following $p,q$ are real numbers, $a,b$ are positive numbers, and $m,n$ are positive integers. $$\begin{aligned} &a^p\cdot a^q=a^{p+q}\\\\ &\dfrac{a^p}{a^q}=a^{p-q}\\\\ &\left( a^p\right)^q=a^{pq}\\\\ &a^0=1,\quad n\ne 0\\\\ &a^{-p}=\dfrac{1}{a^p}\\\\ &(ab)^p=a^pb^p\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{a}=a^{1/n}\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{a^m}=a^{m/n}\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}}=\dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}} \end{aligned}$$ In $a^p$, $p$ is called the exponent, $a$ is the base, and $a^p$ is called the $p\text{th}$ power of $a$. The function $y=a^x$ is called an exponential function.

Logarithms and Antilogarithms

If $a^p=N$ where $a\ne 0$ or $1$, then $p=\log_a N$ is called the logarithm of $N$ to the base $a$. The number $N=a^p$ is called the antilogarithm of $p$ to the base $a$, written $\text{anti}\log_a p$.

Example: Since $4^2=16$ we have $\log_4 16=2$, $\operatorname{antilog_4} 2=16 $

The function $y=\log_a x$ is called a logarithmic function.

Laws of Logarithms

$$\begin{aligned} &\log_a MN = \log_a M + \log_a N\\\\ &\log_a \frac{M}{N} = \log_a M - \log_a N\\\\ &\log_a M^p= p\log_a M \end{aligned}$$

Common Logarithms and Antilogarithms

Common logarithms and antilogarithms (also called Briggsian) are those in which the base $a=10$. The common logarithm of $N$ is denoted by $\log_{10} N$ or briefly $\log N$.

Natural Logarithms and Antilogarithms

Natural logarithms and antilogarithms (also called Napierian) are those in which the base $a=e=2.71828...$. The natural logarithm of $N$ is denoted by $\log_e N$ or $\ln N$

Change of Base of Logarithms

The relationship between logarithms of a number $N$ to different bases $a$ and $b$ is given by: $$\log_a N=\dfrac{\log_b N}{\log_b a}$$ In particular, $$\begin{aligned} &\log_e N = \ln N = 2.30258 50929 94 ... \log_{10}N \\\\ &\log_{10} N = \log N = 0.43429 44819 03 ... \log_e N \end{aligned}$$

Relationship Between Exponential and Trigonometric Functions

$$\begin{aligned} & e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i\sin \theta \\\\ & e^{-i\theta} = \cos \theta - i\sin \theta \\\\ & \sin \theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i} \\\\ & \cos \theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2i} \\\\ & \tan \theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{i\left(e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta} \right)} = -i\left(\frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}} \right) \\\\ & \cot \theta = i\left(\frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}} \right) \\\\ & \sec \theta = \frac{2}{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}} \\\\ & \csc \theta = \frac{2i}{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}} \end{aligned}$$

Periodicity of Exponential Functions

$$ e^{i(\theta + 2k\pi)} = e^{i\theta},\quad k=\text{integer}$$ From this it is seen that $e^x$ has period $2\pi i$.

Polar Form of Complex Numbers Expressed as an Exponential

The polar form of a complex number $z = x + yi$ can be written in terms of exponentials as follows: $$z = x + iy = r\left(\cos \theta + i\sin \theta \right) = re^{i\theta}$$

Operations with Complex Numbers in Polar Form

$$\begin{aligned} & \left( r_1e^{i\theta_1} \right)\left(r_2e^{i\theta_2}\right) = r_1r_2e^{i\left( \theta_1 + \theta_2 \right)} \\\\ & \frac{r_1e^{i\theta_1}}{r_2e^{i\theta_2}} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}e^{i\left( \theta_1 - \theta_2\right)} \\\\ & \left(re^{i\theta} \right)^p = r^p e^{ip\theta},\quad (\text{De Moivre's theorem}) \\\\ & \left(re^{i\theta} \right)^{1/n} = \left[re^{i(\theta+2k\pi)} \right]^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{i(\theta+2k\pi)/n} \end{aligned}$$

Logarithm of a Complex Number

$$\ln \left( re^{i\theta} \right) = \ln r + i\theta + 2k\pi,\quad k=\text{integer}$$
