Laws of Exponents

In the following $p,q$ are real numbers, $a,b$ are positive numbers, and $m,n$ are positive integers. $$\begin{aligned} &a^p\cdot a^q=a^{p+q}\\\\ &\dfrac{a^p}{a^q}=a^{p-q}\\\\ &\left( a^p\right)^q=a^{pq}\\\\ &a^0=1,\quad n\ne 0\\\\ &a^{-p}=\dfrac{1}{a^p}\\\\ &(ab)^p=a^pb^p\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{a}=a^{1/n}\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{a^m}=a^{m/n}\\\\ &\sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}}=\dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}} \end{aligned}$$ In $a^p$, $p$ is called the exponent, $a$ is the base, and $a^p$ is called the $p\text{th}$ power of $a$. The function $y=a^x$ is called an exponential function.

Logarithms and Antilogarithms

If $a^p=N$ where $a\ne 0$ or $1$, then $p=\log_a N$ is called the logarithm of $N$ to the base $a$. The number $N=a^p$ is called the antilogarithm of $p$ to the base $a$, written $\text{anti}\log_a p$.

Example: Since $4^2=16$ we have $\log_4 16=2$, $\operatorname{antilog_4} 2=16 $

The function $y=\log_a x$ is called a logarithmic function.

Laws of Logarithms

$$\begin{aligned} &\log_a MN = \log_a M + \log_a N\\\\ &\log_a \frac{M}{N} = \log_a M - \log_a N\\\\ &\log_a M^p= p\log_a M \end{aligned}$$

Common Logarithms and Antilogarithms

Common logarithms and antilogarithms (also called Briggsian) are those in which the base $a=10$. The common logarithm of $N$ is denoted by $\log_10 N$ or briefly $\log N$.

Natural Logarithms and Antilogarithms

Natural logarithms and antilogarithms (also called Napierian) are those in which the base $a=e=2.71828...$. The natural logarithm of $N$ is denoted by $\log_e N$ or $\ln N$
